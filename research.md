### Work in Progress

<b>Black Box or Sandbox: Computational Approaches to Interpreting Michigan's Clean Slate Law</b>
<br>
<br>
<i>(under review at 3rd ACM Symposium on Computer Science and Law)</i>
<br>
<br>
The U.S. State of Michigan’s Clean Slate Statute, defining conditions under which prior convictions can be removed from an individual’s public record, has allowed thousands of people to lead freer and more productive lives. Yet the complex and evolving nature of the law has led to confusion, disagreement, and uncertainty. The Clean Slate laws went into effect in 2021 allowing individuals to apply for an expungement from the court in which they were convicted. The law also required the Michigan State Police to find a way to automate this process within two years. In compliance with that mandate, the Michigan State Police have implemented and deployed a “Rules Engine” in 2023 that automatically detects some expungeable convictions and removes them from individuals’ records. The Rules Engine has greatly increased the throughput of set-aside cases and scaled up the benefits of the Clean Slate laws, but in its current form it is a black-box solution with minimal explanation for the general public. There is a risk that this automation will diminish individuals’ sense of agency and engender a passive attitude toward their criminal records. Our contributions in this paper are threefold. First, we articulate some of the confusion and disagreement over the interpretation of the Clean Slate statute, through interviews with legal professionals. Second, we explain the operation of the Rules Engine, following the relatively sparse explanation available, and we discuss the consequences of this effective but inscrutable tool. Finally, we present our initial work on an alternative computational approach: a “digital sandbox” for legislators, lawyers, judges, and citizens to define and explore the consequences of legislation. The sandbox environment will allow differing interpretations of statutory law, or various revisions to existing law, to be expressed formally and compared through automated analysis. We see computation as a way not only to expand the benefits of criminal justice reform but also to enhance individuals’ understanding and agency with regard to the law. <i>This paper is coauthored with Ishaq Kothari, Kamau Sandiford, Ali Ebnenasir, Susanna Peters, and Charles Wallace. You can access a draft of the paper <a style="color: #f56a6a; !important" href="https://nathanenglehart.github.io/files/ACM_CS___Law_2024-1.pdf">here</a>.</i>

<b>Are Women an Afterthought? Using Text as Data to Scrutinize Press Coverage of Ohio Gubernatorial Primaries</b>
<br>
<br>
<i>(abstract submitted to 81st Annual Midwest Political Science Association Conference)</i>
<br>
<br>
Recent scholarship suggests growing equanimity in press coverage of women running for elective office relative to men, but it is largely based on general election races. Given the press’ unofficial winnowing role at the primary stage, we believe primaries warrant thorough examination. The 2018 Ohio Democratic and Republican primaries for governor provide an ideal case study  for examining gender-based differences in coverage. Multiple qualified women ran for governor, though none secured their party’s nomination. We use text as data methods to scrutinize over 500 newspaper articles published about the gubernatorial primaries in the state’s top-circulating newspapers. We specifically test the “afterthought hypothesis,” which predicts that women were relegated to second tier status by the press - though not explicitly. Using a human-coded training set, we operationalize afterthought status as: 1) prominence of candidate mentions; and 2) substance of candidate mentions. Supervised classification is subsequently used to measure the relegation of women candidates to afterthought status. Analysis of results suggests that the press’ gatekeeper role impedes the candidacies of women in primaries. Our innovative use of machine learning to detect such differences in coverage is ideal for identifying subtle, yet critical, differences in coverage that would otherwise be missed by traditional coding methods. <i>This paper is coauthored with Melissa Miller, Hannah Cubberley, Rachel Martin, and Max Seeley. A draft of the paper is available on request.</i>
